Major Figures of Cocktail History

Below is a a roughly chronological portrait gallery of the most important individuals from the history of cocktail culture, and why, up until the early 2000s. Click through for more information on each entry.

We’ll be filling each of these in gradually over coming months with imagery, biographical background, and context, so check back from time to time.

Richard Stoughton

The de-facto inventor of the cocktail (but not by that name) in London, 1690s

James Ashley

The first celebrity mixologist

Mrs. Gaywood

Mrs. Gaywood, and the barmaids she supervised, were the first drink mixers of public record

Cato Alexander

Owned and operated the definitive “sporty” American bar during the formative period.

Orasmus Willard

Popularized American iced drinks to an influential audience and shaped standards of bar service

Martha King Niblo

Arguably the first female celebrity bartender in the United States; helped popularize the Sherry Cobbler

Peter Bent Brigham

Created the first “fancy drinks menu”, widely immitated

Charles Dickens

Foremost enthusiast, in letters, of civilized drinking

John George Vennigerholtz

Helped introduce more elaborate German mixology to the American Bar

Joseph Santini

One of the most famous bartenders of the mid-19th Century; invented the Brandy Crusta, a fancy drink

Ellen E. Moon

A most famous Bay Area saloon keeper of the mid-19th Century

John Dabney

One of the most famous bartenders of the formative period

Dick Francis

One of the most famous bartenders of the formative period

Jeremiah P. Thomas

The most famous bartender of the mid-19th Century; author of the first recipe book of the American bar

Leo Engel

The most prominent of early emissaries of the American Bar to London

Edward Spencer Mott

Wrote about drinking, past and present, in turn-of-the-20th-Century London

William Schmidt

The most famous bartender of the late 19th Century

Harry Johnson

Wrote the first book about managing a cocktail program, and invented bar management consulting

Vincent B. Miret Sr.

Progenitor of 20th Century New Orleans cocktail culture; co-created and popularized the Sazerac Cocktail as it is now known

Billy Wilkinson

Progenitor of 20th Century New Orleans cocktail culture; co-created and popularized the Sazerac Cocktail as it is now known

Henry Charles Ramos

One of the most famous American bartenders ever

Charles Mahoney

Managed the most fashionable early 20th Century New York bar and wrote the second book about managing a cocktail program

Charlie Paul

A prominant emissary of American cocktail culture to London

C. F. Lawlor

Left a time capsule-like bar book from a smaller American city

Theodore Proulx

Left behind an insightful cocktail book

William T. Boothby

The dean of Bay Area pre-Prohibition mixologists

George J. Kappeler

Wrote (arguably) the finest 19th Century cocktail book

Louis Fouquet

Oscar Tschirky

Oversaw, as maître d’hôtel, the hospitality and culture of the influential Waldorf-Astoria, including its storied bar

Frank P. Newman

Wrote the most important early French cocktail book

Emilio González

The first cocktail king of Cuban bartenders; commercialized the Daiquiri

Patrick Gavin Duffy

Links the pre-Prohibition American Bar to the post-Prohibition American bar

John Applegreen

Wrote the first professional pocket reference for mixology

Jacques Straub

Compiled one of the main drink references of the early 20th Century

Tom Bullock

First African American to write a cocktail book, the second-to-last major cocktail book prior to Prohibition

Albert Stevens Crockett

Wrote of the personalities and lore of pre-Prohibition bar life at the Waldorf-Astoria

Victor Vaughen Morris

Inventor or popularizer of the Pisco Sour

Ada Coleman

First famous female bartender in a cocktail program

Hans Schönfeld

Co-wrote the primary document of pre-war German mixology

John Leybold

Co-wrote the primary document of pre-war German mixology

Harry Craddock

Wrote the best-selling Savoy Cocktail Book and co-founded the UKBG

Jacob A. Grohusko

Earliest prominent Jewish cocktail bartender

Eddie Woelke

One of the most famous bartenders of the 20th Century in the United States and Cuba

Louis Muckensturm

Wrote a distinctive cocktail book that included the first Dry Martini recipe (by that name) in the USA

Hugo R. Ensslin

Wrote a wildly influential cocktail book as Prohibition loomed

Frank Meier

One of the most famous bartenders, ever

Stanley Clisby Arthur

First author to show interest in the origins and history of individual drinks

Crosby Gaige

Post-prohibition taste-maker

Gustav Selmer Fougner

Invented drink writing as we’ve known it since Prohibition

David A. Embury

Wrote the first theoretical work on mixed drinks

Jose Abeal y Otero

Created and operated one of the most famous and enduring cocktail bars of all time

Constantino Ribalaigua Vert

Proprietor of El Floridita, dean of Cuban bartenders, instigator of the frozen Daiquiri

Harry McElhone

One of the most famous and productive bartenders, bar owners, and mixologists ever; owned and operated Harry’s Bar in Paris

José Cuervo Fernández

Co-founded the Club de Cantineros de Cuba, served as its first President, and co-wrote its first book

Robert Vermeire

Wrote the best-selling cocktail book, which was one of the first to integrate new European cocktails those from the America

Ted Saucier

Kept cocktail culture vital in New York society during 1950s and 1960s

Charles H. Baker Jr.

Wrote the “Here’s How” column in Gourmet, yielding two of the most distinctive books on mixed drinks

Miguel Boadas

The most famous Catalan bartender of the 20th Century

Pedro Chicote Serrano

Most famous bartender of mid-Century Spain

Ernest Hemingway

Celebrity drinks enthusiast and influence

J. Popo Galsini

One of the most famous West Coast bartenders of the Cold War era; invented the Saturn cocktail

Lucius Beebe

Post-prohibition taste-maker

Victor Bergeron

Founded Trader Vic’s exotic restaurant chain, created the Mai Tai

Oscar Haimo

Relentlessly documented the shifting tastes in post-Prohibition cocktails, and founded one of the first post-Prohibition professional organizations

William J. Tarling

One of the most famous English bartenders; co-founded the UKBG

Mariano Licudine

The creator of the drinks program at the Mai Kai, Ft. Lauderdale

Ernest Raymond Beaumont Gantt

Invented the “exotic drink” and the establishments in which they were served

Raymond Realista “Ray” Buhen

Key bartender in the history of Tiki bars and drinks

Jacinto Sanfeliú Brucart

Stalwart of mid-Century Madrid mixology

Joe Scialom

One of the most famous 20th Century bartenders and mixologists

Cora Irene Sund

Built out Don the Beachcombers as a real business, expanding it across the United States

John Durlesser

Popularized the Margarita; dean of California bartenders

Joseph Harold Baum

Critical link between the pre-Prohibition American Bar and the Cocktail Renaissance in the United States

Luigi Veronelli

An inspiration for the “Slow Food” movement in Italy, which in turn significantly influenced the Cocktail Renaissance; also wrote multiple books on cocktails

María Dolores Boadas

The most famous Spanish bartender of the latter 20th Century

Alan Stillman

Created T.G.I. Friday’s and the party bar

Stan Jones

Wrote the only important drink book of the 1970s

Charles Schumann

Created the Cocktail Renaissance in Germany through his Munich bar and publications

Alice Waters

Creditable, by proxy, with defining the overarching culinary context and values for mainstream contemporary cocktails

Dale DeGroff

Progenetor of the Cocktail Renaissance in the United States

William Grimes

Researched and encapsulated the history of the cocktail at the outset of the Cocktail Renaissance

Gary Regan

Anticipated the Cocktail Renaissance and was, with Dale DeGroff, its early leader; wrote The Joy of Mixology

Anistatia Miller

Championed the rediscovery of the history of the cocktail

Ted Haigh

The most prominent of the enthusiasts and collectors to help launch the Cocktail Renaissance in its eary years

Salvatore Calabrese

Primed London for the Cocktail Renaissance

Robert Toomey

Promoted interest in cocktails and connected enthusiasts cross-country in the years leading up to the Cocktail Renaissance

Jeff Berry

Singlehandedly recovered the recipes and stories of many lost exotic drinks such as the Zombie, and their creators

Dick Bradsell

Progenitor of the Cocktail Renaissance in London

Robert Hess

The facilitator of the Cocktail Renaissance its early years

Tony Abou-Ganim

Highest-profile mixologist in the United States in the 1990s

Jonathan Downey

The entrepreneur of the London Cocktail Renaissance

David Wondrich

Researched and decoded the history of spirits and mixed drinks for the Cocktail Renaissance

Audrey Saunders

The most famous mixologist of the Cocktail Renaissance; trained multiple generations of bartenders for the Cocktail Renaissance

Toby Cecchini

Created the Cosmopolitan (as it became known)

Jared Brown

Championed the rediscovery of the history of the cocktail

Ann Tuennerman

Created Tales of the Cocktail

Shinichi Ikeda

Brought Japense cocktail culture to New York City at Angel’s Share bar

Ted Breaux

Creditable with bringing back absinthe, largely extinct since the early 1900s

Paul Harrington

Arguably, launched the Cocktail Renaissance

Julie Reiner

Created and operated the first high-volume cocktail-centric drinks program of the Cocktail Renaissance

Sasha Petraske

Created Milk & Honey in New York and trained a couple generations of bartenders for the Cocktail Renaissance
