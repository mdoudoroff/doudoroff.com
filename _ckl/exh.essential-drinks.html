Essential Drinks of the American Bar

This exhibit is a fairly complete list of the drinks that—at one point or other—achieved ubiquity and fame. These are the drinks that mixed drinks and cocktails are (or were) known for generally, by people not personally invested in mixology. Consequently, this list is dismal for emphasizing the famous, infamous, and fashionable, regardless of quality—and several drinks on this list are arguably not good drinks! The other problem this sort of reductive list poses is that it overlooks many thousands of drinks, some of which are amazingly delicious, shortchanging the fertile creativity and adaptability that is mixology.

Many of these drinks are wonderful. Moreover, they’re major protagonists in the history of mixed drinks, they help to illustrate the story (please also see our timeline), and most have stories of their own.

Many of these aren’t just one drink, either. Some are entire categories. We have even had to invent a few categories to show how the sprawling world of cocktails can be more organized than one might assume.

Two additional notes: first, we have omitted a smattering of late-20th Century drinks that have been popular but aren’t mixologically interesting, such as the mimosa, the screwdriver, and various “shooters”. These aren’t irrelevant, but other drinks can stand in for them. Second, there are no recent drinks, here. The Cocktail Renaissance that began in the 1990s has produced about twenty “modern classics” and a great many other delicious drinks, but it’s too soon to know which ones fit into this particular company, and the answer may prove to be none.

punch

spirits, citrus, sugar, water, and often spices

The foundational mixed drink, emerging in British-occupied India in the 16th Century, and across the colonial world by 1660.

 

purl

ale or wine infused with wormwood (and sometimes other botanicals)

A common, rudimentary English digestive aid dating from the 1600s

Sang-gris

Madeira wine, sugar, citrus and spices

A spiced Madeira punch, probably from England, by the 1690s

 

Stoughton’s Elixir

medicinal extract of various botanicals

The original bitters, a “stomach settler”, manufactured and marketed by Richard Stoughton, c. 1690, London; a great success in authentic or imitation form well into the 19th Century; the bitters used in the original American Cock-Tail, but also a proto-cocktail when simply added to beer or wine, as was the original function

 

hot spiced/buttered rum

rum, hot water, sugar, butter and spices

A pleasant, warming Colonial drink whose popularity has waxed and waned but never gone away

 

sangaree

strong wine (but sometimes beer or even spirits), sweetened and diluted with water, often with a spice element or a float of something

A branch of punch evolved from Sang-Gris that worked its way around the colonial Atlantic eventually diversifying into a slightly shifty category of drinks by the 1820s, the Port Wine Sangaree being the most successful

 

toddy

spirits, sugar, and water (often hot, sometimes cold)

Initially a simple whisky preparation from Scotland, quickly popular in British colonial America, where it was adapted, diversified and was often known as the Sling

Ice Punch

punch (most commonly rum punch) with ice in it for chilling and dilution

As ice first began to transition from a luxury item to a commodity in the 1780s, iced punch began to be reformulated with ice in mind and was advertised as such

 

sling

spirits, sugar and (usually cool) water (and maybe nutmeg)

The American toddy, maybe a bit stiffer, maybe less likely to be served hot, maybe more likely to be topped with nutmeg, but commonplace in the early 19th Century

 

Pousse Café/Chasse Café

brandy or liqueur, or a mixture or layering of them

Began as a French tradition, by the 1780s, of a brandy or liqueur served after the meal; by the 1850s, the bourgeoning class of professional bartenders in America transformed it into a “stunt drink” that showed their mastery of their ingredients and technique

 

Mint Julep

spirits (rum or brandy initially, whiskey after the Civil War), sugar, water (ice) and mint (and sometimes a float of this or that)

One of the earliest and most essential American drinks, begun in Virginia by the 1780s, but fully realized by 1810, with the addition of ice, leading to universal popularity until Prohibition; currently underappreciated

Egg Nogg

brandy and rum (or some other selection of spirits), eggs, sugar, cream and nutmeg

A classic American drink decended from the Colonial posset and the flip, first entering the written record in 1788 in New Jersey and Pennsylvania newspapers

 

Cocktail (or Cock-Tail)

spirits (most commonly a genever, rye whiskey or brandy), sugar, water and bitters

A “bittered sling” that emerged at the outset of early 19th Century as a hangover cure, delivering a stiff-but-palatable hair-of-the-dog with “medicine” (the bitters); ubiquitous by the 1930s

 

collins

spirit, sugar, lemon juice and carbonated water

Created in a London coffee house, c. 1807, by John Collin, the refreshing “Collin’s punch”, made with gin, was later popularized in Australia, Canada and America in the 1860s in both genever (John Collins) and tom gin (Tom Collins) forms before spawning other variations with other spirits.

 

cobbler

wine (less commonly, spirits), sugar, ice (generally shaken with, and garnished with fruit)

A key class of American mixed drink, apparently originating in New York, and one of the most popular from the 1840s–1900, particularly in the form of the Sherry Cobbler; also connected to the popularization of the drinking straw, due to its coldness

 

smash

spirits (brandy, genever or rye whiskey), mint and sugar syrup

A simple class of drink obviously evolved from the julep, popular from at least the 1840s until around 1900

 

highball

spirits, carbonated water and ice

Too simple and effective to not be popular, with ubiquity growing alongside the rise of mechanical carbonation in the 19th Century

 

Sherry Cobbler

sherry, sugar, ice (generally shaken with, and garnished with fruit)

One of the most popular drinks of the 19th Century (by 1838), and second only to the julep for the popularization of iced drinks

 

Champagne Cocktail

champagne, sugar and bitters

The principal cocktail with champagne, closely tied to emergence of modern Champagne in the mid-19th Century, and where sparkling wine jumped from the punch bowl to the single serving glass

 

fix

spirits (typically genever or Old Tom), lemon juice, sugar, ice, and maybe a touch of liqueur

A class of mixed drink from the 1850s—a simple, single-serving punch of served American-style in a small bar glass like a Cock-Tail, with a fruit garnish; antecedant of the Bramble

 

Crusta

brandy (or less commonly genever or rye whiskey), sugar, curaçao, lemon juice and bitters

A drink with an unprecedentedly fussy presentation, created by Joe Santini, c. 1850-1855, in New Orleans, but even more important for introducing citrus juice to the Cocktail

 

sour

spirits, sugar, lemon juice (and sometimes egg white and/or a splash of carbonated water)

An adaptation of punch to the smaller, single-serving, personalized template of the American bar, by the 1850s

Pisco Punch

pisco, lemon juice, pineapple juice, sugar (and possibly cocaine)

Punches made from Peruvian brandy (Pisco) were known from the 1830s, and were a defining phenomenon at the heart of pre-Prohibition San Francisco drinking culture from the 1850s

 

Caipirinha

cachaça, sugar and lime

The most famous Brazilian batida and the form that achieved world fame, existed, in practice, by 1856, near Rio, but not by name until the 1950s

 

swizzle

spirits and crushed ice, usually with a sweetener like sugar or falernum, sometimes with lime juice

The use of a swizzle stick (a suitably straight twig with a crotch of trimmed boughs at one end whose stubs serve as paddles) to mix and froth a drink date to the Caribbean colonies of the 1600s; by the 1860s, this regional tradition was married to growing availability of ice

 

Vermouth Cocktail

vermouth (Italian, usually), gum syrup, bitters (and sometimes maraschino liqueur or other additions)

Somehow, in the late 1860s, the mysterious ethnic specialty that is vermouth began to fascinate the American bar, and the first application was evidently the direct substitution of the strong wine for the spirits in the established Cocktail template

 

cocktail [americano]

spirits, aperitif/digestif wine or liqueur, and sometimes other additions

This is an invented category to corral the wildly successful branch of cocktails that incorporated European aperitif and digestif products in addition to or in place of aromatic bitters; this category begins with the adoption of Italian and French vermouths by American bartenders in the late 1860s, the Manhattan and Martini being the most famous examples, and extends through today’s fascination with Italian amari

 

Gin & Tonic

gin, carbonated tonic water, and a lime or lemon squeeze

One of the most ubiquitous drinks in the world, emerged in 1860s India, achieving world-wide popularity by the 1930s

 

Improved Cocktail

spirits (genever, cognac or rye), gum syrup, maraschino, bitters and absinthe

Here, in the 1870s, we see the Cock-tail diversify into an infinitely variable, generic class of drink, simultaneous to cocktail culture entering mainstream embrace

 

fizz

spirits, citrus, sugar and sparkling water (and often other additions)

A class of mixed drink from the 1870s, achieving great popularity in the 1880s, spawning many variations and deviations; traditionally smallish, and strained rather than served over ice

 

daisy

spirits (originally gin or whiskey); curaçao, grenadine and/or raspberry liqueur; lemon juice or lime juice, and carbonated water

A class of mixed drink—a Sour sweetened and flavored with a liqueur or fruit syrup—from the 1870s that enjoyed two bouts of popularity prior to Prohibition, making the “new school” transition to the cocktail glass alongside the sour in the 1890s, then reemerging after Prohibition as one of the most popular drink categories, led by the Sidecar and Tequila Daisy (the Margarita)

 

flip

egg (whole), sugar, and spirits (and sometimes nutmeg or cream), shaken vigorously with ice and strained

A class of rich American mixed iced drink from the 1870s taking its name from a Colonial-era drink but perhaps more direct inspiration from Egg Nogg; the form enjoyed some popularity until World War II, particularly the Brandy Flip and Sherry Flip

 

cooler

spirits, citrus peel, ice, carbonated water or ginger ale (and sometimes other things)

An influential, but vague class of American drink close to the Highball, less sweet and less sour than a Collins, and always featuring carbonated water or ginger ale

 

Manhattan

rye whiskey, sweet vermouth and bitters, stirred and strained

The second major vermouth drink, by 1882, New York, where Italian/sweet vermouth and American whiskey share space in the Cocktail and produce a notable effect greater than their mere sum

 

Rickey

spirits (American whiskey or gin), lime juice, carbonated water and ice

A particular unsweetened style of Cooler or Highball developed in the 1880s and named for ‘Colonel’ Joe Rickey that enjoyed great popularity in the 1890s and early 1900s

 

Martini

gin (Old Tom or London Dry) and vermouth (sweet or dry), and sometimes bitters, absinthe or other additions

The king of cocktails began inauspiciously, in the 1880s, with numerous vermouth experiments less successful than the Manhattan, mostly built with the genever that was commonly called “gin” in America; the increasing availability of Old Tom gin and later, London Dry gin, in the 1890s transformed the drink, revealing a new, remarkable synergy between the mysterious vermouths from Italy and France and these English gins; countless variations ensued under a bewildering number of names, but the underlying idea was here to stay, with the Dry Martini (below) ultimately dominating

 

Bamboo

sherry, vermouth and orange bitters

Created by bartender Louis Eppinger, San Francisco, by 1886, followed by many other sherry-and-vermouth drinks by other names (Adonis, Harvard, etc.)

 

Old Fashioned

rye or bourbon, sugar and bitters

At some point in the 1880s, it became common in Chicago to refer to the original “bittered sling” Cock-tail as the “old fashioned Cocktail”, or even just the “old fashion”, to distinguish it from the flood of newer drinks also called cocktails; this new name stuck

 

Ramos Gin Fizz

Old Tom gin, cream, lemon juice, lime juice, egg white, sugar, carbonated water and orange flower water

In the early 1890s, New Orleans bartender Henry Carl Ramos combined the Silver Fizz, the Cream Lemonade (from the pharmacy soda fountain world), and possibly the influence of the local Milk Punch into a new kind of fizz that has endured to the present day

 

Dry Martini

dry gin and dry vermouth (and maybe a dash of orange bitters and/or maybe absinthe)

The most iconic cocktail, evolved in 1890s New York with the arrival of London dry-style gins from England and the discovery of their affinity with the dry French vermouth style

 

cocktail [sour]

spirits, lemon or lime juice, and sugar (and sometimes other additions)

This is an invented category for the “new school sours” after around 1890 that were just called cocktails instead of being called sours; these were designed around and served in the cocktail glass; of many examples, the most important example is the Daiquiri; see also Daisy

 

cocktail [dessert]

This is an invented category to organize a diverse array of sweet drinks that began to present as cocktails in the 1890s, but have their roots in European after-dinner drinking traditions such as the Parisian pousse café or Italian scaffa; famous examples include the Stinger, Alexander, Grasshopper and Irish Coffee

 

Sazerac

rye whiskey, simple syrup, Peychaud’s Bitters, absinthe (and sometimes maraschino liqueur)

The particular way of serving the Improved Whiskey Cocktail developed at the Sazerac House in the 1890s that went on to great fame; distinguished by an absinthe-rinsed glass and the use of the local Peychaud’s Bitters

Café Brûlot

cognac, kirchwasser, coffee and spices

The vanishingly rare example of an “extravaganza” cocktail service that achieved longevity

 

Daiquiri

Cuban-style rum, sugar and lime juice

The most iconic and important cocktail sour, prototyped by Yankees at a iron mine near Santiago in 1896 and subsequently refined and popularized by Havana bartending legends Emelio “Maragato” González and Constantino “Constante” Ribalaigua Vert

 

Singapore Sling

gin, Cherry Heering, Bènèdictine, lime juice, carbonated water and Angostura bitters

The American sling evolved through England and the British colonies first to resemble a cooler or collins, then, by 1910, in Singapore, acquiring liqueurs, a name, and global popularity

 

Rob Roy

blended scotch, sweet vermouth and bitters

A Manhattan made with blended scotch, but far and away the most popular scotch cocktail; by 1895, Hoboken, New Jersey

 

Americano

amaro, vermouth and carbonated water

A family or template of drinks, late 1890s, where the American cocktail employing Italian ingredients (vermouth) is re-interpreted by the Italians, substituting their regional aperitif and digestif bitters (amari) for the aromatic bitters (such as Bokers or Angostura) used in America

 

Jack Rose

applejack, lime juice and grenadine

One of the other great daisies, and the most popular cocktail featuring applejack (or American apple brandy)

 

Clover Club

gin, lemon juice, raspberry syrup and egg white

Enormously popular, visually striking cocktail from the 1901–1920, revived in 2007 by Julie Reiner

 

Cuba Libre

light rum, Coca-Cola and lime juice

The ubiquitous “rum & Coke”, often improved with a squeeze of lime juice, possibly begun around 1900, but certainly established by 1920, and immensely popular curing World War II

 

Gibson

dry gin and French vermouth (later, a pickled onion garnish became standard)

The first fetishistic Dry Martini, from the Bohemian Club, San Francisco, 1898; tenaciously popular

 

Ward 8

rye whiskey, lemon juice, sugar, grenadine (and sometimes orange juice, curaçao, and/or amontillado)

A daisy created by 1905, Boston, and particularly popular in the 1930s–1940s

 

Bronx

dry gin, dry vermouth, red vermouth, Angostura bitters, and orange

Emerged in New York City—possibly the Waldorf-Astoria Hotel—circa 1900–1907 and enjoyed great popularity until Prohibition; the first popular cocktail to feature orange juice

 

Rose

kirschwasser, French vermouth and siro de groseille

An essential, distinctive, and resolutely French drink popular by 1910 until World War II; the foremost cocktail built around an eau-de-vie

 

Ohio

often brandy, often whiskey, often white wine, usually champagne on top, maybe some vermouth, some kirsch, some bitters or dashes of liqueurs, but no citrus juice

The Ohio Cocktail is a German cocktail—perhaps the German cocktail—dating to the late 1890s, with many recipes spanning the ensuing decades, but few consistent characteristics; it is generally spiritous, and often topped with champagne, which can’t help but make it celebratory in some sense

 

El Presidente

Cuban-style light rum, Chambéry blanc vermouth, curaçao and grenadine

The second Cuban classic, invented by 1915, possibly by Constante Ribalaigua in Havana, introducing the still-new Chambéry blanc-style vermouth to mixology

 

Stinger

brandy and crème de menthe

Brandy and crème de menthe was an after-dinner drink mixture as early as 1890, but it became fashionable as the Stinger in 1913

 

Aviation

dry gin, lemon juice, maraschino liqueur and crème de violette

Apparently created by obscure New York bartender Hugo Ensslin c. 1916, boucing around Europe during Prohibition, and enthusiastically embraced at the start of the Cocktail Renaissance

 

Tomato Juice Cocktail

 

Negroni

gin, red vermouth, Campari (and sometimes carbonated water)

By 1920, Florence, an Americano variation strengthened with gin and featuring the then-novel Campari aperitif bitters; enjoyed multiple periods of popularity and proved particularly influential to the Cocktail Renaissance

 

Brandy Alexander

brandy, crème de cacao and heavy cream

The foremost cream drink, and one of the most popular drinks from the 1920s through the end of the 20th Century

 

Sidecar

Cognac, triple sec, lemon juice

The most famous cognac cocktail, and the second-most famous “new school” Daisy (to the Margarita); by 1922, France (possibly the French Riviera)

 

White Lady

dry gin, triple sec, lemon juice (and sometimes egg white)

The most elemental “new school” Daisy, popular by 1929, London

 

Pisco Sour

pisco, lemon juice or lime juice, sugar, egg white, and bitters

A sour, formulated around the Peruvian brandy, served at Morris’ Bar, Lima, by 1924, and achieving world-wide fame in the 1960s

 

Mojito

Cuban-style rum, lime juice, sugar, mint, and carbonated water

A cooler or collins made with Cuban rum and mint, this drink can be traced to Prohibition, where it was popular with Americans on a drinking vacation in Havana, and then again in the United States during the 1990s, when it enjoyed a vigorous resurgence beside the Cosmopolitan

 

French 75

dry gin or brandy, lemon juice, sugar and champagne

Tenaciously popular, and the rare classic cocktail to emerge in American during Prohibition

 

Zombie

 

Vodka Martini

 

Margarita

tequila, triple sec, and lime juice

The (perhaps) most popular cocktail in the world apparently began as a Tequila Sidecar (amongst other names) in the 1930s at the hands of mixologists in various places, finally gaining its name in the late 1940s; from there, the drink was everything to tequila marketing and, thus impelled, became many things

 

Bloody Mary

vodka, tomato juice, lemon juice, worcestershire sauce and Tabasco sauce

One of the most ubiquitous mixed drinks in the world, born of the rise of canned tomato juice in the 1920s as a hangover helper

 

Moscow Mule

 

Mai Tai

 

Irish Coffee

Irish whiskey, sugar, hot coffee and whipped cream

One of the most ubiquitous mixed drinks in the world, created c. 1944 by chef Joe Sherican in Foynes, Ireland, and popularized in San Francisco’s Buena Vista Café in the 1950s

 

Piña Colada

 

Blue Hawaii

vodka, light rum, blue curaçao, pineapple juice and sour mix

Created for Bols’ marketing of their blue-colored curaçao, ushered in the blue drink era and interesected the (rum-heavy) world of exotic drinks with the post-war vodka craze

 

Harvey Wallbanger

vodka, orange juice and Galliano

Enormously popular drink in the 1970s driven by marketing and enabled by its simplicity and flavor novelty

 

Lemon Drop

 

Tequila Sunrise

 

Long Island Iced Tea

 

Cosmopolitan

Absolut Citron, Cointreau, lime juice and cranberry juice

The transitional cocktail that prepared the market for the Cocktail Renaissance; popularized by 1990 at Odeon, New York City, with the help of Absolut marketing and the drink’s instantly-recognizable appearance, made world-famous by 2000 through the “Sex and the City” television show

 

Appletini

vodka, sour apple liqueur and triple sec

A late-1990s popular phenomenon that galvanized (in opposition) the Cocktail Renaissance, and fed a period of burgeoning menus in mainstream bars and restaurants where every drink was suffixed by -tini; from Lola’s Restaurant, West Hollywood, c. 1996-1997, following the introduction of DeKuyper Sour Apple Pucker

 

Aperol Spritz

Aperol, prosecco and an orange slice

The Spritz, a Venetian regional specialty since the early 20th Century, explodes world-wide in 2003 on the back of aggressive Aperol marketing

 

Planter’s Punch

 

 

 

 

 

 

[exotic drinks]

spirits, citrus and fruit juices, various sweeteners and spices

What came to be known as “exotic drinks” was almost entirely invented in Hollywood by Donn Beach, at his Don the Beachcomber restaurant: he created extraordinarily sophisticated variations on Caribbean Planter’s Punch, and presented them with the trappings of fantasy escapism, drawing particularly from Polynesian language, art, and aesthetics; success and imitators followed; almost all exotic drinks are some form of punch, and the most famous examples are the Zombie and Mai Tai
