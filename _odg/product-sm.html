Old Duff Genever 100% Maltwine

What it is

Old Duff Genever 100% Maltwine with the Seal of Schiedam is an homage to the style of genever which spread all around the world from the 1500s to the late 1800s, which is when more and more Dutch distillers began using neutral spirit as well as maltwine. Only three 100% maltwine genever brands in existence qualify for the Seal of Schiedam.

To make Old Duff Genever 100% Maltwine, we first mill rye and barley grains in De Vrijheid (The Freedom) windmill, right outside distillery De Tweelingh (“The Twin”) in the historic Dutch distilling town of Schiedam. De Vrijheid was built in 1785, and milling still occurs using wind power and milling stones.

Next we ferment a mash of ⅔ rye and ⅓ malted barley for five days, then distill it—with all the grains as well as the fermented liquid in the pot-still—three times, to create what is called maltwine.

A small portion of this maltwine is then redistilled with a tiny amount of juniper, and another portion is redistilled with a similarly small amount of English Bramling hops, then all three maltwines—maltwine, juniper maltwine and hop maltwine—are combined. Nothing else is added but water to adjust the strength to 45% abv, and then it is bottled.

Seal of Schiedam

To be awarded the Seal of Schiedam—a paper label sealing the bottle, countersigned by the mayor of Schiedam since 1902—a 100% maltwine genever must be made in a designated distillery in Holland, based only on grains, distilled only in a pot-still for a minimum of three times, and be bottled at between 43% abv and 48% abv. Additionally, the distillery must be used only to pot-distill genever—it is not permitted to have any other kind of still than a pot-still in the building! Only two such designated distilleries still exist, both in Schiedam—De Tweelingh, and the Genever Museum itself, housed in a former distillery less than half a mile away from De Tweelingh.

Why do we do it this way?

We mill the grain in De Vrijheid windmill because that’s how it was always done—it might be cheaper or more efficient to use a modern mill with a digital readout, but that’s not how genever was made hundreds of years ago. We use lots of rye because it creates an incredible flavour and is historically accurate, even though rye is a difficult grain to work with. Rye has a tendency to foam when fermenting, and requires a long, expensive fermentation period of five days as opposed to 48 hours for most other grains. Rye began to fall out of favour with Dutch distillers in the mid-1700s when they gained access to wheat and corn that were both cheaper and easier to work with. Likewise, hops were once very common in genever, but began to be overshadowed by more glamourous botanicals such as citrus peels and nutmeg, brought back to Holland in the 1600s and 1700s on the ships of the Dutch East India Company (VOC) and the Chartered West Indies Company (GWIC).
