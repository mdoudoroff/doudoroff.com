About Us

Old Duff Genever was created by Philip Duff, a native of Skerries, a small seaside town about twenty miles north of Dublin in Ireland. A lifelong bartender whose work had already taken him to Dublin, London, the Cayman Islands and New York, in 1995 Philip accepted a job offer in Rotterdam, Holland, on a three-month contract. Philip overstayed that contract by seventeen years (!), buying a house near Rotterdam and mastering the language as well as opening Holland’s first-ever speakeasy. door 74, in Amsterdam became the first-ever Dutch bar in the World’s 50 Best Bars list and at one time was ranked #15 in the world. Philip also founded the Liquid Solutions consultancy to help drinks brands work better with bars and as part of a project involving the creation of a genever brand for the gigantic Bols company in 2008, he learned that almost no genever company in Holland actually distilled its own genever anymore -they all bought it in, most of it from a single distillery in Belgium. Delicious stuff, usually - but deceptive. This was the birth of the idea that would become Old Duff Genever: a genever that was milled, mashed, fermented, distilled and bottled in Holland, transparent about its origins, and would help revive the almost extinct category of 100% maltwine genevers with the Seal of Schiedam.

And the goat?

Well, genever bottles like the Old Duff Genever bottle are called kelderflessen, meaning “cellar bottles” - and are the oldest type of glass genever bottles. The shape almost demands a round label at the top of the bottle. Because back in the day genever sold everywhere from Accra to Buenos Aires to Shanghai, an animal would often be pictured in the label, to communicate brand values for people who often couldn’t read English or Dutch. An elephant for a genever that was strong-tasting, a monkey for something spicy - you get the idea. Well, Philip’s hometown Skerries in Ireland was once home to someone else - St. Patrick, who was a Christian missionary at the time and not yet a saint. One day, when he was away, the locals stole his goat, killed it and ate it - these are documented facts. The legends begins with what happened next: when St. Patrick found his goat was missing, he asked the locals what had happened and they lied, so he took their voices and replaced them with the bleating of a goat. And to this day, if you’re a native of Skerries, you’re called a goat - and that’s why there’s a goat on the Old Duff Genever label.
