What is Genever?

Genever is a Dutch word for a distilled spirit with juniper and derives from the French word for juniper, genièvre; when you say genever (or jenever) in Dutch, its assumed you are referring to the distilled spirit, whereas juniper berries are referred to as “geneverbessen / jeneverbessen”. Genever was spelled with a “g” and pronounced “jin-EE-vur” from the 1500s until the early-to-mid 1800s; by 1840 “jenever” (yuh-NEE-vur) began to be used more commonly instead.

Genever is a delicious grain spirit based on pot-still maltwine with a very slight touch of juniper and optionally some other botanicals too. Botanicals in genever function like bitters in an Old Fashioned cocktail; they add minute hints of depth and flavour, but never dominate. Genever can be composed of 100% maltwine, or can contain neutral spirits as well as maltwine, in which case it is variously named corenwyn/korenwijn (“grain wine”, minimum 51% maltwine), oude (“old-style”, minimum 15% maltwine) or jonge (“new-style”, minimum 1½% maltwine, maximum 15% maltwine).

How Its Made

Under EU regulation 787, genever may only be made in Holland, Belgium, the départements Nord and Pas-de Calais in France, and the regions Nordrhein-Westfalia and Niedersachsen in Germany.

Genever is made by fermenting what is usually a mixture of several grains (typically corn, wheat and barley), then distilling the resulting mash a minimum of three and a maximum of four times in pot stills to make a distillate named moutwijn (maltwine), which must be between 46% and 48% abv. after the third distillation. The result of the first distillate is named ruwnat (“rough liquid”), the second enkelnat (“single liquid”) and the third bestnat (“best liquid”), which can also be called maltwine. Then, either some of that maltwine, or some neutral alcohol from a column still is redistilled with juniper berries, plus (optionally) any other botanicals desired. Historically, only minute amounts of botanicals were used, and generally only a small number of botanicals as well. The original maltwine distillate is then combined with the botanical maltwine, plus any sugar or neutral alcohol (which are both optional) and it is then reduced to bottling strength with water and bottled. Genever is not required to be aged, but if aging is mentioned on the label, it must have been aged for a minimum of 1 year in barrels no larger than 700 liters.
