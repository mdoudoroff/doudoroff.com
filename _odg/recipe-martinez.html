1883: The Martinez

First mentioned in print in 1883 in Chicago, the Martinez cocktail is an important part of the evolution of cocktails in general. That 1883 recipe—which describes the Martinez as being made with gin or whisky—only makes sense if you know how close in flavour oude genever and whisky are; “gin” here means “Dutch gin”—i.e., genever. By 1884, it was generally accepted that a Martinez was made with genever and a Manhattan was made with whiskey. These early first Martinez/Manhattan recipes often used twice as much sweet vermouth as genever, which is our recommended proportion too. Eventually, the Manhattan proportions reversed to become twice as much whiskey as sweet vermouth. In due course, when dry vermouth began to be popular in the US around the 1900s, bartenders experimented by making Manhattans and Martinezs with this new dry vermouth; unfortunately, dry vermouth does not combine well with flavourful grain distillates like whiskey or genever. So another new-ish import, English dry gin, was pressed into service, possibly because older bartenders remembered that the Martinez was made with (Dutch) gin; this new cocktail of English dry gin combined with dry vermouth came to be called the Martini. Thus the Martinez and Manhattan are parents of the Martini.

The Martinez

2 shots (60ml) sweet vermouth
1 shot (30ml) Old Duff Genever 100% Maltwine
1 barspoon (5ml) maraschino liqueur
1 barspoon (5ml) dry curacao liqueur
3 dashes of Boker’s/Bogart’s aromatic bitters

Stir with ice
Strain into a chilled cocktail coupe
Garnish with 3 speared gourmet cherries and a lemon zest.
