Genever Mixology

When faced with a genever cocktail recipe, it is usually safe to assume that its based on oude genever, that is, genever with 15% or more maltwine content. If the recipe is from before the 1950s its probably a genever with 50% or more maltwine (a/k/a Corenwyn/korenwijn) and if it is from before 1900 or so it refers to 100% maltwine genever. Recipes with jonge genever tend to be from Holland and date from the 1970s to the present day.

History

If you were in the mood for spirits and walked into almost any decent bar in the world between about 1500 and 1945, genever would have been one of the default orders. Initially drunk neat and often accompanied by a beer in its homelands, its first fame in mixed drinks was as one of the original spirit bases of punch together with arrack, brandy, rum and (later on) whisky. Punch, an English invention, was the world’s mixed drink of choice from its birth around 1670 until it handed the baton to the cocktail in the 1850s, “cocktail” being a new way of mixing drinks born in America. Genever was beloved of these new American mixologists too; fully 15 of the recipes in the world’s very first cocktail book, Jerry Thomas’ 1862 Bartender’s Companion, were genever based, and the harbour of New York alone imported 450 bottles of Dutch genever in the mid-1800s for every 1 bottle of English gin. After WWII, jonge genever began to crowd out oude in Holland.

Mixing with Genever

Because genever’s botanicals add only subtle hints of flavour, mixing with genever the same way you would with gin—using botanicals as a jumping-off point—can sometimes be a dead end when it comes to creating new genever cocktails. Instead, cocktail-wise it might be better to think of genever as a whisky; taste it, learn the mashbill, and work out which flavors you want to emphasise or complement. A high-rye genever (like, ahem, Old Duff Genever) contributes powerful, spicy rye notes that blossom into a broad, warm palate and a long finish. Genever based on a maltwine containing mostly wheat or corn will be softer, sweeter and richer, as will genevers that contain sugar. Aged genevers will have barrel influence, everything from the citrus-vanilla common to ex-bourbon barrels to the dried fruit aromas and viscosity imparted by ex-sherry barrels. Unaged genevers with higher maltwine percentages – Korenwijn and 100% maltwine – often have aromas described as funky, like rhum agricole, mezcal or cachaca. Jonge genever functions like vodka, providing an almost-neutral “stage” for other ingredients to perform upon.
