Everyone is hopefully arriving Friday, March 17, or earlier.

Thursday, March 23

  • No agenda, dine/swim/relax as you like at the Old Cataract Hotel.

Friday, March 24

  • 11:00 am Pick-up from the hotel lobby by SEEgypt agent and Fatma and transfer to board the SANCTUARY SUN BOAT III (moored in a private dock in Aswan), where we will enjoy a welcome Zaffa (musical procession with drums and flutes).
  • lunch will be served on board
  • This afternoon we will ride on a felucca (a typical Egyptian sail boat) around Elephantine Island, Lord Kitchener’s Botanical Gardens, and the Agha Khan Mausoleum (weather permitting; life jackets will be provided).
  • Afternoon tea will be served in the lounge.
  • Tonight, we will be greeted by our Boat Manager for canapés, accompanied by local Egyptian wine and beer. Dinner on board follows.

Saturday, March 25

  • A breakfast buffet will be served on board.
  • Then, we will take a short motorboat ride to visit the romantic and majestic Philae Temple on the Island of Agilika.
  • This will be followed by a visit to the Granite Quarries, which supplied the ancient Egyptians with most of the hard stone used in pyramids and temples, and still hold a huge unfinished obelisk.
  • Later, an Egyptian cooking lesson will be presented on board.
  • Enjoy lunch, while we cruise to Kom Ombo and visit the magnificent Temple of Kom Ombo, dedicated to the crocodile-God Sobek.
  • Afternoon tea will be served while we cruise to Edfu.
  • In the evening, dinner is served, followed by a movie night on board.

Sunday, March 25

  • A breakfast buffet will be served on board.
  • This morning, we explore the extraordinary Temple of Horus.
  • Once we return to the boat, we continue to enjoy our cruise onward to Esna, while enjoying lunch.
  • This afternoon, Fatma will guide us around the Greco-Roman Temple of Khnum at Esna. The beautifully preserved Great Hypostyle Hall was built during the reign of the Roman Emperor Claudius; it was excavated from the silt that had accumulated through centuries of annual Nile floods, and is about nine meters below present-day street level.
  • Afternoon tea will be served while we sail to Luxor.
  • Tonight will be an “Egyptian Night” costume party, with a chance to dress up in traditional Egyptian “galabeyya” style. Dinner will consist of a lavish buffet of Egyptian specialties, followed by oriental music and dancing.

Monday, March 27

  • Breakfast buffet will be served on board.
  • This morning, visit the Valley of the Kings. We will have the chance to visit at least one tomb in the Valley of the Queens Or Medinet Habu, and visit the Temple of Queen Hatshepsut. On the way back to the river Nile, we will pass by the famed Colossi of Memnon, known in Ancient Greek times for their haunting voices at dawn.
  • OPTIONAL TICKETS: Tomb of Queen Nefertari ($105 per person) / Tomb of Seti 1st ($105 per person) / King Tut in Luxor ($25 per person) through A&K
  • Lunch on board follows
  • Then, Fatma will guide us around the strikingly graceful Temple of Luxor, dedicated to the god Amun.
  • Then, we return to the boat for afternoon tea in the lounge.
  • Tonight’s entertainment will be a gala dinner, with white-gloved waiters serving gourmet cuisine followed by a Dervish Show and belly dancer performance. And, a birthday cake for Chris.

Tuesday, March 28

  • A buffet breakfast will be served on board, before check out at 8am from our cabins
  • Then, we will visit Karnak Temple
  • 12:00pm depart to Luxor airport to fly back to Cairo: Egyptair Flight MS 067 Luxor to Cairo 2:20pm depart, 3:30 pm arrive (Terminal 3)

Bibliography

Martin found these books useful:

  • Ancient Egypt: The Definitive Visual History (2021) — large “coffee table”-style book, but organized into easily digestible bits, basically chronological, extensive visual references, handy reference to ancient Egyptian dieties
  • The Rise and Fall of Ancient Egypt: The History of a Civilisation from 3000 BC to Cleopatra (2010) — dense-but-readable history

Ancient Egyptian History from 30,000 feet

(This is Martin’s attempt to reduce 3,000 years of history to a most basic framework for the sake of memory.) By coincidence, the four major dynastic eras of ancient egypt almost fit into neat 500 year chunks, with 100 years (give or take) of instability and strife at the end of each.
